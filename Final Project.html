Biography

Dr. Shirley Ann Jackson is a theoretical physicist and black inventor has made many advances in science. As a child she first developed an interest in science and math and conducted several experiments and studies. She attended Roosevelt High School and graduated as valedictorian in 1964. She later went on to study at MIT. As a student she tutored students at the Roxbury YMCA and volunteered at the Boston City Hospital. Although she was accepted into Brown, Harvard, and the University of Chicago, Dr. Jackson chose to stay at MIT for her doctoral work.

She has a Ph.D. in Theoretical Elementary Particle Physics and an S.B. in Physics. In the 1970s, she studied and conducted research at physics laboratories in Europe and in the United States. Her first official job was as a research associate at the Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory. There she studied hadrons which are subatomic particles that inclue baryons and mesons. In 1976, Dr. Jackson joined the Theoretical Physics Research Departments at AT & T Bell Laboratories. There she examined the properties various materials in an effort to discover useful applications. She also explored theories of charge density waves and the reactions of neutrinos.

Dr. Jackson has led a world-class technological research at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute of Troy, New York, and Hartford, Connecticut. She has a vision to remake the university and it was known as The Rensselaer Plan. The transformation included a Center for Biotechnology and Interdisciplinary Studies, the Curtis R. Priem Experimental Media and Performing Arts Center, and the Center for Computational Innovations.

Accomplishments

First African American woman to earn a Ph.D. in theoretical solid state physics at MIT.

Appointed as chairwoman of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission in 1995.

President Obama presented her with the National Medal of Science in 2014.

18th President of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute.
